Part 1 — Background

What Is a Neural Network?

A neural network is a mathematical system loosely inspired by the human brain. It consists of neurons — simple computational units — organised in layers. The first layer receives input data; the last layer produces an output (a prediction or classification); intermediate layers, called hidden layers, perform internal transformations. Neurons between adjacent layers are connected by weights — numerical values that determine how strongly one neuron influences the next.

Learning happens through a process called training. The network is shown many examples with known correct answers. For each example it makes a prediction, compares it to the correct answer, measures the error, and then adjusts its weights slightly to reduce that error. Specifically, if increasing a weight would have made the prediction worse, that weight is reduced; if increasing it would have made the prediction better, it is increased — each by a small amount proportional to its contribution to the error. This adjustment propagates backwards through the network — hence the term backpropagation. After thousands of repetitions the weights settle into values that allow the network to make accurate predictions on new, unseen data. The network does not follow explicit rules written by a programmer; it discovers its own internal patterns entirely from the data it is trained on.

Part 2 — The Demo

What You See on the Page

The page is divided into two areas. On the left is a live visualisation of the neural network itself. Circles represent neurons, arranged in four vertical columns corresponding to four layers: the input layer (4 neurons), two hidden layers (6 and 4 neurons), and a single output neuron on the right. The lines connecting neurons represent weights.

Before training, the lines are thin and nearly uniform — the weights are random and the network knows nothing. During training, watch the lines change: they grow thicker as weights strengthen, and shift in colour — warm golden tones indicate a weight that pushes the risk score upward, cool blue tones indicate a weight that suppresses it. The rightmost neuron gradually shifts colour from green (low risk) toward red (high risk) as the network learns to discriminate.

On the right panel you will find three sections. The Training Data table shows the ten labelled contracts the network learns from — each described by four features: whether it contains a penalty clause, its duration, the number of parties, and the degree of ambiguous language. Five contracts are labelled HIGH risk and five LOW risk.

The Training section contains a button to start training and a progress bar. Below the bar a small chart plots the prediction error over time — you should see it fall steeply at first, then flatten as the network converges. The epoch counter shows how many training iterations have completed.

Once training is finished, the Try It section becomes meaningful. A Yes/No toggle controls the penalty clause feature; three sliders let you set contract duration, number of parties, and degree of ambiguous language. The network instantly produces a risk score — a percentage — and a verdict. Try toggling the penalty clause on and off, or setting all sliders to maximum — the risk score should jump accordingly. You can also press Reset and train again: the result will differ slightly each time, because the initial weights are chosen at random.

Part 3 — Key Concepts

What This Illustrates

Weights & Learning
The visible thickening and recolouring of connections is the actual learning process, not a metaphor for it.
Black-Box Opacity
The network produces a number, but there is no simple rule to read off why. This opacity is central to AI regulation and explainability law.
Generalisation
Trained on ten contracts, the network scores any combination of inputs — including ones it has never seen. Whether it generalises correctly depends on the training data.
Reproducibility
Press Reset, then Train again. The same architecture, the same data, a different outcome — a challenge for reproducibility in legal and regulatory contexts.
Discussion point: This network was trained on ten hand-crafted examples. Real legal AI tools are trained on millions of documents. The fundamental mechanism is identical — but the opacity, the potential for bias, and the difficulty of auditing scale up with it.
