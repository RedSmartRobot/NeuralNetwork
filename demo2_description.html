Part 1 — Background

The Black Box Problem

When an AI system reaches a conclusion, it does so through millions of internal numerical operations that no human directly controls or reads. The system was not programmed with rules like "if penalty clause and long duration, then high risk" — it inferred its own internal logic from training examples. This makes it a black box: inputs go in, outputs come out, but the reasoning in between is opaque.

One important consequence of this opacity is sensitivity to phrasing. Unlike a human lawyer who understands that "breach," "violate," and "fail to perform" are legally equivalent, an AI system processes language statistically. Slight changes in wording shift the statistical patterns the system recognises, and can therefore shift the output — sometimes dramatically — even when the underlying facts are identical.

This has direct legal implications. If an AI tool is used for contract review, risk assessment, sentencing recommendations, or evidence analysis, the party who controls how facts are described to the system has significant influence over the output. The system itself provides no warning that this has happened.

Part 2 — The Demo

What You See on the Page

The page presents four pre-built scenarios, each covering a different area of law: contract breach, tort liability, criminal intent, and employment discrimination. Each scenario consists of two prompts — Prompt A and Prompt B — that describe the same underlying facts using different language or emphasis. When you select a scenario, both prompts are sent simultaneously to a real AI (Claude), and the two responses appear side by side.

Below each response you will see two measured scores. These are not calculated by a separate system — they are values the AI itself reports when asked to self-assess its response in a structured format:

Confidence
How certain the AI says it is in its assessment, on a scale of 0–100. A score of 85 means the AI considers the answer fairly clear-cut; a score of 40 means it sees the question as genuinely ambiguous.
Risk Signal
How strongly the AI's response points toward legal risk, liability, or an adverse finding, on a scale of 0–100. A high score means the AI leaned toward finding a problem; a low score means it leaned toward finding none.

Below the two response cards, an Output Divergence meter shows the average gap between the two scores. A high divergence on semantically equivalent prompts is the central demonstration: the same AI, the same facts, a different answer.

At the bottom of the page you can enter your own prompt pair and run the comparison live. Try rephrasing the same legal question from a plaintiff's perspective and then a defendant's perspective, and observe the effect.

Part 3 — Key Concepts

What This Illustrates

Input Sensitivity
AI outputs are a function of exact input tokens, not underlying meaning. Small lexical changes can cross decision boundaries inside the model.
Framing Effects
Whoever drafts the prompt to an AI system exercises significant influence over its output — without the system disclosing that influence.
Auditability
If the same tool gives different answers to equivalent questions, how should a court assess its reliability? Current evidentiary standards were not designed for this.
Consistency
Human legal standards require consistent application of rules. AI systems have no built-in guarantee of consistency across paraphrased inputs.
Discussion point: If an AI risk-assessment tool is admitted as evidence, should the opposing party be entitled to test it with alternative phrasings of the same facts? What standard of adversarial testing should be required before AI output is treated as reliable?
