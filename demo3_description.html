Part 1 — Background

AI Hallucinations in Legal Research

Large language models generate text by predicting, word by word, what a plausible continuation of a given prompt would look like. They do not look things up in a database, and they do not know what they do not know. When asked a question whose answer lies outside their training data — or simply at the edge of it — they continue generating text anyway, producing plausible-sounding but factually false content. This phenomenon is called hallucination.

Legal citations are a particularly dangerous area for hallucination. A well-formed citation — Smith v. Jones, 412 F.3d 88 (2d Cir. 2005) — follows a predictable grammatical pattern. A language model can produce thousands of syntactically correct citations that refer to cases that have never existed. The model has no internal mechanism to distinguish "I know this case exists" from "this is what a citation looks like."

This is not hypothetical. In 2023, New York attorneys Roberto Mata and Steven Schwartz submitted a legal brief in Mata v. Avianca containing six AI-generated case citations. None of the cases existed. When the court asked for copies, the attorneys produced fabricated decisions. Judge P. Kevin Castel sanctioned both attorneys and the law firm, noting that the citations had been "gibberish" and that counsel had abandoned their professional obligations by submitting AI output without verification.
Part 2 — The Demo

What You See on the Page

Select one of the five pre-built legal research queries, or type your own at the bottom. The application makes two sequential AI calls:

Step 1 — Generate. The AI is asked to answer the legal question with a short paragraph and exactly four case citations. Critically, it is instructed to mix real and fabricated citations without labelling which is which — mimicking exactly what happens when a lawyer uses an AI research tool without adequate instructions.

Step 2 — Verify. A second AI call takes the four citations and checks whether each one actually exists. Each citation card then flips from "CHECKING…" to one of two states:

✓ VERIFIED The AI has reasonable confidence this case exists in legal records and the citation details are plausible.
✗ FABRICATED The citation does not appear to correspond to any real case. The name, reporter, or court details were invented.

The running totals at the top of the page track how many citations have been checked across all queries in your session, and what proportion were fabricated. Run several queries to build up a meaningful sample.

Important note on the verification step: the second AI call is itself subject to the same limitations. It may occasionally mark a real case as fabricated, or vice versa. This is intentional — it reinforces the point that you cannot reliably use AI to fact-check AI. Independent verification against an authoritative legal database (Westlaw, LexisNexis) remains essential.

Part 3 — Key Concepts

What This Illustrates

Hallucination
AI generates fluent, confident text regardless of factual accuracy. There is no "I'm not sure" signal — false and true citations look identical.
Professional Duty
Counsel has an independent duty to verify any citation submitted to a court. Delegating research to AI does not discharge that duty.
Confidence ≠ Accuracy
The authoritative tone of AI output is a property of the generation process, not evidence of correctness. Hedged language and false confidence look the same.
Verification Gap
AI cannot reliably verify its own output. External, authoritative sources remain the only trustworthy check on AI-generated legal research.
Discussion point: Several bar associations have now issued guidance requiring attorneys to verify AI-generated citations. Should verification be a formal procedural requirement — analogous to signing a pleading — before AI-assisted research can be filed with a court? Who bears liability if a fabricated citation goes undetected?
